## Prompt

Some people think that universities should only offer subjects that will be useful
in the future, such as those related to science and technology. To what extent do
you agree or disagree?

## Essay (≈ 270 words)

It is sometimes argued that universities ought to teach only subjects with obvious
practical value, particularly in science and technology. While I accept that such
fields are vital to a modern economy, I disagree with the idea that other
disciplines should be abandoned.

Those who support a narrow, vocational model of higher education have a reasonable
starting point. Economies increasingly depend on scientific research and
technological innovation, and graduates in these areas tend to find well-paid jobs
and contributes directly to national development. From this point of view, public
money spent on degrees with no clear career outcome can look like a waste.

However, this argument overlooks the wider purpose of a university. Subjects such
as history, philosophy and literature train students to think critically, to weigh
evidence and to communicate persuasively — skills that employers in every sector
value highly. Moreover, a society that educates only scientists and engineers risks
losing the artists, writers and thinkers who give a culture its identity. The 2008
financial crisis, for instance, was caused partly by a failure of judgement, not by
a shortage of technical knowledge.

For these reasons, I believe universities should continue to offer a broad range of
subjects. Practical disciplines clearly deserve strong support, but the humanities
and social sciences develop qualities that are just as important to a healthy
society. A balanced system, rather than a narrowly useful one, serves both the
economy and the wider community best.
