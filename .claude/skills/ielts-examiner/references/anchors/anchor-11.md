## Prompt

Some people think the purpose of a university education is to prepare graduates for
the workplace. Others believe its true value lies in the pursuit of knowledge for its
own sake. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.

## Essay (≈ 295 words)

The purpose of higher education has long been contested. While some maintain that
universities exist primarily to equip graduates with employable skills, others insist
that their deeper mission is the pursuit of knowledge for its own sake. Although both
positions have merit, I ultimately believe the two aims are complementary rather than
opposed.

Those who champion the vocational view make a compelling practical case. A degree
represents a substantial investment of time and money, and graduates understandably
expect it to improve their prospects. Disciplines such as medicine, engineering and
law are, by their very nature, oriented towards professional competence, and it would
be perverse to train a surgeon without reference to the operating theatre. A
university that ignored the labour market entirely would, on this argument, be failing
the very students who fund it.

Yet to reduce education to job preparation is to mistake a by-product for the goal.
Universities have historically been places where ideas are questioned and curiosity is
cultivated for its own sake, and it is precisely this freedom that has produced the
discoveries on which modern economies now depend. A graduate who has learned to think
critically and to tolerate uncertainty is, in any case, far more adaptable than one
drilled in skills that may be obsolete within a decade.

In my view, these objectives reinforce one another. The disciplined habits of mind
fostered by genuine scholarship are themselves the most valuable asset a university
can confer on an employee, while the demands of professional life lend abstract
learning its urgency and direction. The most successful institutions, therefore, are
not those that choose between the two, but those that refuse to.
